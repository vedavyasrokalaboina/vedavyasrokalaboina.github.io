Thrust Vector Control Using Jet Vanes

Conducted an in-depth investigation into the aerodynamic performance of multiple supersonic nozzle geometries as part of a study on thrust vector control using jet vane technology. The project focused on analysing how different nozzle exit shapes influence the behaviour of supersonic exhaust flow and its effectiveness in enabling thrust vectoring for aerospace propulsion systems. Six different nozzle geometries were examined, including circular, rectangular, hexagonal, triangular, square, and elliptical configurations. The analysis involved simulating supersonic exhaust flow at approximately Mach 1.8 exiting the nozzle and studying the development of the jet core downstream of the nozzle exit. The jet core region represents the portion of the exhaust plume where the flow velocity remains nearly constant before significant mixing with the surrounding atmosphere occurs. By evaluating the length of this jet core region for each nozzle geometry, the study aimed to determine which configuration provided the most stable and efficient supersonic flow characteristics. Using aerodynamic and fluid dynamics analysis techniques, the core length was measured from the nozzle exit to the point where the Mach number began to decay significantly due to mixing and turbulence. A comparative analysis of the six nozzle geometries revealed that the rectangular nozzle configuration produced the longest jet core length among the tested designs. A longer jet core indicates a more stable and coherent supersonic jet, which can enhance thrust vectoring effectiveness and overall propulsion efficiency. The primary objective of this project was to explore the feasibility of implementing jet vane-based thrust vector control systems for missiles or aircraft propulsion systems. Unlike traditional aerodynamic control surfaces such as rudders or elevators, thrust vectoring directly manipulates the direction of the exhaust jet to generate control forces. This approach is particularly valuable in high-speed or high-altitude flight regimes where conventional control surfaces may become less effective. By evaluating different nozzle geometries, the study aimed to identify configurations that maximise jet stability and improve the performance of thrust vector control systems for advanced aerospace applications.
